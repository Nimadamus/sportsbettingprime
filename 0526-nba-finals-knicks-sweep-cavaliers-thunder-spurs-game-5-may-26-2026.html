NBA Finals 2026: Knicks Sweep Cavaliers to Reach First Finals Since 1999, Await Thunder-Spurs Game 5

May 26, 2026 | NBA | Sports Betting Prime

For the first time in twenty-seven years, the New York Knicks are going to the NBA Finals. They did not back into it, either. They swept the Cleveland Cavaliers out of the Eastern Conference Final in four straight games and slammed the door with a 130-93 demolition in Game 4 that was over by the third quarter. The franchise that last reached this stage in 1999 has done it again, and the only question left in the league is who will be standing across from them, because the Western Conference Final is still a coin flip with the Oklahoma City Thunder and San Antonio Spurs knotted at two games apiece heading into Game 5 in Oklahoma City on Monday night.

Eastern Final: New York Closes It in Four

The sweep did not feel like a sweep until you look back at how it started. Game 1 went to overtime, with the Knicks edging Cleveland 115-104 in the extra period after the Cavaliers pushed them to the brink in regulation at Madison Square Garden. That was the warning. New York found a way to win the close one, and once a series gives a hungry team the tight game, the floodgates tend to open. They did. The Knicks took Game 2 by a 109-93 margin, controlled Game 3 on the road 121-108, and then turned Game 4 into a statement, burying Cleveland 130-93 to finish the series without a single loss.

Jalen Brunson was the engine the whole way, the kind of two-way, big-moment guard who has turned this Knicks group from a plucky overachiever into a genuine title contender. The story around him all postseason has been whether a Brunson-led championship would make him the greatest Knick of the modern era, and a Finals berth only turns that conversation up louder. What matters from a series standpoint is that New York never let Cleveland breathe. The Cavaliers had spent the regular season building a reputation as an analytics darling, and the Knicks answered the talk by winning the only metric that counts four times in a row.

Why the Sweep Matters Beyond the Banner

A four-game sweep is not just a result, it is a gift of time. The Knicks now get nearly a week of rest while the Western Final grinds on, and that recovery window is enormous for a team that leans heavily on its starters. Brunson and the New York core get to heal bumps, sharpen the game plan, and watch two exhausted Western contenders beat each other up for at least one more game, possibly three. In a league where Finals are often decided by which team arrives fresher, the Knicks just bought themselves the most valuable currency in the playoffs.

There is also a psychological dividend. Sweeping a conference final, rather than surviving a seven-game war, lets a team carry momentum and belief into the championship round without the scar tissue of a near-death series. The 130-93 finish was the kind of performance that tells a locker room it belongs. New York is not happy to be here. New York thinks it can win the whole thing, and the manner of this series gives that belief a foundation.

Western Final: Thunder and Spurs Deadlocked at 2-2

While the East was being decided, the West has refused to settle. Oklahoma City and San Antonio have traded haymakers through four games, and the series sits tied 2-2 with the swing back in the Thunder's hands for Game 5. San Antonio drew first blood in Game 1, stunning Oklahoma City 122-115 in double overtime, the kind of marathon opener that announces a series will go the distance. The Thunder steadied themselves with a 122-113 Game 2 win and then took control on the road with a 123-108 Game 3 result that had the look of a team seizing the series.

Then San Antonio punched back, and hard. The Spurs throttled Oklahoma City 103-82 in Game 4, a defensive clinic built around the rising force of Victor Wembanyama, whose two-way impact has been the central story of the Western Final. Holding any team to 82 points in a conference final is a statement, and it dragged the series back to even. Now everything funnels into Game 5 in Oklahoma City on Monday, May 26, at 8:30 p.m. ET, a true pivot point where the winner takes a 3-2 lead and a commanding grip on the path to face the waiting Knicks.

What Game 5 Decides for the Finals Picture

The contrast in how these two conference finals have played out is the most interesting subplot of the entire postseason. The Knicks are rested, healthy, and confident after a clean sweep. Their eventual opponent will be road-weary and bruised, having gone at least five brutal games in a series with two double-digit blowouts and a double-overtime opener already in the books. That gap in mileage is exactly the kind of edge that decides a championship before the first Finals tip.

For Monday night specifically, the home floor matters. Oklahoma City reclaims the building for Game 5, and the Thunder will want to make the Spurs pay for the Game 4 blowout by reestablishing the pace that won them Games 2 and 3. San Antonio, with Wembanyama anchoring a defense that just suffocated the Thunder, has every reason to believe it can steal a road game and push Oklahoma City to the brink. Whichever team emerges, it walks into a Finals against a New York club that has had a week to prepare, rest, and stew on the chance to end a championship drought that stretches back to 1973. Game 5 does not just decide a conference. It sets the terms of the Finals matchup the entire league has been waiting on.
