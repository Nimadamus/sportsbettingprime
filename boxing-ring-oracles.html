The Undisputed Heavyweight Championship Rematch

Tyson Fury and Oleksandr Usyk meet for the second time in seven months, with all four heavyweight championship belts on the line. In their first encounter on May 18, 2025, Usyk won by split decision (115-113, 114-113, 113-114) in one of the greatest heavyweight fights of the 21st century. Fury controlled the early rounds with his jab and movement, but Usyk rallied in the middle rounds and nearly finished Fury in the 9th with a barrage of punches that had the "Gypsy King" stumbling. The judges' scorecards were controversial, with many media outlets scoring it for Fury. This rematch will determine the greatest heavyweight of this era.

Fury's Size and Reach Advantage

Tyson Fury stands 6'9" with an 85-inch reach, making him one of the biggest heavyweights in boxing history. He weighed 262 pounds for the first fight, using his size to lean on Usyk in the clinch and tire him in the later rounds. Fury's jab is his most effective weapon — it's long, fast, and disrupts opponents' rhythm. When Fury is on his game, he uses lateral movement, feints, and angles to frustrate smaller opponents who can't reach him with their punches. However, at 37 years old, Fury has shown declining footwork and stamina in recent fights, particularly in rounds 9-12.

Usyk's Technical Brilliance and Footwork

Oleksandr Usyk is a former undisputed cruiserweight champion who moved up to heavyweight and dominated the division with his superior boxing skills. The Ukrainian southpaw is a technical wizard who uses footwork, angles, and timing to outmaneuver larger opponents. In the first fight, Usyk's body attack in rounds 6-9 slowed Fury down, and his combination punching in the 9th round nearly ended the fight. Usyk's stamina is elite — he's never faded in the championship rounds and actually gets stronger as fights progress. His ability to adjust mid-fight and exploit opponents' weaknesses makes him one of boxing's smartest fighters.

The Adjustment Period: Who Learns More?

In rematches, the fighter who makes better adjustments typically wins. Fury's trainer, SugarHill Steward, will likely emphasize more aggressive body work and sustained pressure in the middle rounds — the period where Usyk took over the first fight. Fury needs to control the pace and avoid the lulls that allowed Usyk to build momentum. Usyk's team will study Fury's early-round strategy and prepare counters for his jab and movement. The key question: can Fury maintain his early-round success for all 12 rounds, or will Usyk's adjustments and stamina overwhelm him again in the championship rounds?

The Judges and Venue

The fight takes place in Saudi Arabia, where judging has been controversial in recent high-profile bouts. In the first fight, one judge scored it 115-113 for Fury despite him nearly being stopped in the 9th — a questionable scorecard that suggests potential bias or incompetence. If this fight is close, the judges could once again be the deciding factor. Fury's team will likely instruct him to be more aggressive and leave no doubt, while Usyk needs to dominate rounds clearly to avoid a potential robbery on the cards.

Cardio and Championship Rounds

Fury has shown cardio issues in his last three fights, fading noticeably in rounds 10-12. His weight (262 lbs in the first fight) suggests he's carrying extra mass that could tire him as the fight progresses. Usyk, meanwhile, weighed 223 lbs and looked fresh in the final rounds of their first encounter. If this fight goes the distance, Usyk's superior conditioning will be a massive advantage. The over/under of 9.5 rounds reflects the expectation of a decision — neither fighter has been knocked out in their professional careers.

Betting the Underdog with Value

Tyson Fury at +140 offers value despite losing the first fight. Many observers (including this analyst) scored the fight for Fury based on his control of the early rounds. If Fury can implement his game plan and avoid the 9th-round collapse, he has the tools to win a decision. However, Usyk's technical superiority and championship-round dominance make him the safer bet at -165. The smart play is to take Fury at +140 as a live underdog and pair it with OVER 9.5 rounds, expecting a competitive decision that could go either way. If you want the safest bet, take Usyk by decision at +140 — he wins the championship rounds and takes a close decision on the cards.

Pound-for-Pound King vs. The Young Lion

Terence "Bud" Crawford is widely considered one of the top 3 pound-for-pound fighters in the world. The 37-year-old Omaha native is a complete fighter with elite skills, power in both hands, and the ability to switch seamlessly between orthodox and southpaw stances. Crawford's resume includes dominant wins over Errol Spence Jr., Shawn Porter, and Amir Khan. Jaron "Boots" Ennis is the 27-year-old rising star from Philadelphia, known for his explosive power and aggressive fighting style. Ennis has knocked out 30 of 33 opponents and holds the IBF welterweight title. This is a classic passing-of-the-torch matchup.

Crawford's Ring IQ and Adaptability

Crawford's greatest asset is his fight IQ — he can diagnose opponents' weaknesses mid-fight and adjust his strategy accordingly. In his fight against Errol Spence Jr., Crawford started slowly in rounds 1-3 before systematically breaking Spence down with body shots and switching stances. Crawford's jab sets up everything: he uses it to measure distance, disrupt rhythm, and set up power shots. His defense is underrated; he slips punches with minimal head movement and counters with precision. At 37, Crawford is at the tail end of his prime, but his experience and technical mastery still make him a nightmare matchup for anyone.

Ennis' Youth and Explosive Power

Jaron Ennis is a volume puncher with devastating power — he throws 70-80 punches per round and has a 91% knockout rate. His left hook to the body is particularly dangerous, often setting up fight-ending uppercuts. Ennis fights with controlled aggression, pressuring opponents while maintaining defensive responsibility. His youth and athleticism give him a physical edge over Crawford, who has shown declining foot speed in recent fights. If Ennis can pressure Crawford and make him fight going backwards, his power could be the difference.

Experience vs. Prime Athleticism

This fight pits Crawford's experience against Ennis' prime athleticism. Crawford has been in big fights against elite competition, while Ennis' resume includes solid but not elite opposition (Karen Chukhadzhian, David Avanesyan). When the lights are brightest and the pressure is highest, Crawford's experience could overwhelm Ennis mentally. However, if Ennis can stay composed and implement his pressure game plan, his physical prime could overcome Crawford's declining athleticism.

The Betting Play

Crawford at -280 is expensive, but he's the superior fighter with proven championship pedigree. Ennis at +220 offers value as a live underdog with knockout power. The safest bet is Crawford by decision or late stoppage, but the under 8.5 rounds offers intriguing value at +115 if Crawford can break Ennis down like he did Spence. Primary play: Crawford by KO in rounds 9-12 at +180.
