The Knicks Are Drawing Nearly Half The NBA Finals Money. Here Is What That Lopsided Split Actually Tells You.

June 1, 2026 | NBA | Sports Betting Prime

One number cuts through all the noise around the 2026 NBA Finals, and it has nothing to do with rest, home court, or Victor Wembanyama. At one major New York sportsbook, the Knicks have drawn nearly half of all the money wagered on the NBA Finals winner this season, almost as much as every other team in the league combined. That is not a betting market. That is a fan base with a credit card. For anyone trying to read the Knicks-Spurs series price honestly, that single split is the most important context there is, because a number that lopsided distorts everything posted on top of it.

Geography Is Doing A Lot Of The Work

Before reading anything predictive into that handle, start with where it was measured. This is a New York sportsbook reporting that New York bettors are pounding the New York team. That is not a market signal so much as a home-market effect, the well-documented tendency for a book's handle to tilt heavily toward the local rooting interest regardless of the underlying value. A Knicks ticket in Manhattan is part wager and part merchandise, and a meaningful slice of that nearly-half share is emotional money rather than sharp money. The first job in reading any split this extreme is separating the location of the book from the wisdom of the bet, and at a New York book backing the Knicks, the location explains a great deal on its own.

This matters because a national handle would almost certainly look different. If the Knicks were drawing close to half of all Finals money across the entire country, that would be a far stronger statement about public conviction. Concentrated at one regional book in the team's home city, the same figure is more a measure of local passion than of a continent-wide read on who wins the title. Treat the number as a thermometer for New York enthusiasm first, and only second as any kind of comment on the Spurs.

Handle Is Not The Same As The Smart Side

The deeper point is one every serious bettor eventually internalizes: the percentage of money on a team is not the same as the percentage of correct money on a team. Books do not move lines simply because a lot of tickets land on one side. They move lines to manage risk and to respect the bets that tend to be right, and those two groups, the loud majority and the sharp minority, frequently sit on opposite sides of the same number. When a single team commands nearly half a book's Finals handle, the operator is not panicking. It is doing exactly what it is built to do, taking heavy public action on a popular side and pricing accordingly.

That dynamic is the entire foundation of contrarian, or fade-the-public, thinking. The logic is not that the crowd is always wrong, because it is not. The logic is that when money piles onto one side for reasons that include geography, brand, and emotion, the price on that side can drift past its true value, and the other side can quietly become the better number. A Knicks futures price shaped by half the room betting from the team's home city is precisely the kind of number worth examining skeptically rather than chasing, and the Spurs side is where that skepticism naturally points.

What This Does To The Series Price

Tie it back to the matchup we broke down in our full Finals preview. On the floor, this is a close, genuinely uncertain series: a rested New York team that controls its own freshness against a San Antonio team with home court and a transcendent young star in Wembanyama. A coin-flip on the court does not square with one side commanding nearly half the handle off it. That gap between an even matchup and a wildly uneven betting split is exactly where market inefficiency tends to live.

None of this is a prediction that the Knicks lose. They are an excellent team with a real path to a title, and heavy public money lands on the eventual champion all the time. The takeaway is about process, not outcome. When you see a split this extreme, do not let it function as evidence that the Knicks are the smart bet, because a number inflated by a home crowd is not proof of anything except enthusiasm. Read the handle for what it is, weigh it against the actual matchup, and let the lopsidedness make you more curious about the Spurs price, not less.

The Read Heading Into Game 1

The Knicks drawing nearly half of all NBA Finals money at a New York book is a fascinating data point and a textbook example of why handle alone is a trap. It tells you New York is fully invested, both emotionally and financially, in a team back in the Finals for the first time since 1999. It does not tell you the Knicks are the value. As Game 1 approaches on June 3 in San Antonio, the disciplined move is to treat that split as a reason to scrutinize the price rather than trust it, to remember that a home-market book reports a home-market lean, and to ask whether all that one-sided money has nudged the Spurs into the better number. The crowd has spoken loudly. The smart question is always whether the crowd has spoken correctly.
