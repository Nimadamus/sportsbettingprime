The Tigers Team-Total Steam Is The Sharp Story Of Sunday's MLB Board

June 7, 2026 | MLB Team Totals | Sports Betting Prime

Line movement is the cheapest tell in this business, and Sunday morning it spoke loudly in Detroit. The Tigers team total opened at 4.5 and got bet straight down to 3.5 before most of the country had finished its coffee. That is not a rounding error or a weather adjustment. A full run of movement on a team total is the market telling you that money it respects believes one side of this lineup is going to be quiet, and when you go digging into why, the run rates and the pitching matchup line up behind the steam almost perfectly. The Tigers are the headline, but they are not alone. Four home teams on the June 7 board are sitting on team-total unders that the numbers actually support, and that is the kind of clustered signal worth slowing down for.

We track this stuff for a reason. A team-total under is one of the cleanest bets in baseball because it strips away half the variance. You do not need to care what the opposing offense does, what the bullpen on the other side coughs up, or whether the game goes to extras and balloons the full-game total. You are betting one thing: this specific lineup, against this specific starter, in this specific park, does not get to a number. When the line steams in your direction and the season-long run rate agrees with it, you have the two things that matter pointing the same way.

Detroit Tigers Under 3.5: The Steam And The Run Rate Agree

Start with the move itself, because the move is the story. The Tigers team total opened at 4.5 Sunday and got hammered down to 3.5, with the under sitting at plus money around even. That is an aggressive number for a home team, and the market did not arrive there by accident. Over their last 30 days, the Tigers have averaged just 3.33 runs per game across 27 contests. They have been held to three runs or fewer in 17 of those 27 games, a 63 percent clip, and to four or fewer in 21 of 27. This is not a lineup that has been mashing and got caught by a bad number. This is a club the market has correctly identified as one of the quieter offenses in the league over the past month, and the steam down to 3.5 is the books catching up to that reality.

The matchup reinforces it without leaning on the names. Seattle sends Luis Castillo, whose surface ERA of 5.53 looks ugly but undersells the swing-and-miss in his profile, with 56 strikeouts in 55.1 innings and a strikeout-per-nine north of nine. Over his last five starts he has mixed in a four-run clunker against the White Sox with a scoreless four-inning outing against the Athletics and a one-run, five-inning effort against Arizona on May 31. He is volatile, but he is the kind of arm who can carry a quiet lineup through five and hand the ball off before the offense ever gets going. Against a Detroit club averaging 3.33 a night, the under 3.5 is the bet the smart money already made for you.

The tell: Tigers team total opened 4.5, steamed to 3.5 Sunday morning. Detroit is averaging 3.33 runs per game over its last 27 contests and has scored three or fewer in 63 percent of them. The line and the run rate are saying the same thing.

Houston Astros Under 4.5: A Volatile Bat Meets A Tough Profile

Houston is the one that asks for a little more nuance, and that is exactly why we like the number at 4.5 laying minus 125. The Astros have averaged 4.36 runs per game over their last 28, which sits right on the edge of the total, but the shape of that number is what matters. They have been held to four or fewer in 18 of 28 games, a 64 percent rate, with a handful of double-digit explosions inflating the average. A lineup that posts a 13 and an 11 inside its last ten games but also lays four ones and a zero in the same stretch is a boom-or-bust offense, and boom-or-bust offenses are how you find value on a team-total under at a fair price.

The opposing arm tilts it further. Oakland gives the ball to Gage Jump, who has only two big-league starts but has been efficient in both: five innings of four-run ball against Seattle, then seven innings of one-run, three-hit baseball against the Cubs on June 2 with a 1.17 WHIP across the small sample. A fresh arm the Astros have not seen a third time, working with that kind of WHIP, is precisely the profile that keeps a streaky lineup off the board early. Under 4.5 at minus 125 prices in the volatility, and the 64 percent under rate over the last month says it should be closer to a coin flip than that.

Toronto Blue Jays Under 4.5: Baz Has Been Rolling

Toronto's case is built more on the arm coming the other way than on a cold Blue Jays bat, though the offense cooperates too. The Jays have averaged 4.25 runs per game over their last 28 and have been held to four or fewer in 15 of them. Their last ten reads like a team stuck in the 3-to-6 band: a 2, a couple of 3s, a 5, a 6. That is a profile that lives right around the total, which is exactly where you want to be when the pitcher on the mound has been pitching to his ceiling.

Baltimore's Shane Baz has quietly been one of the steadier arms in the division. His season line shows a 4.29 ERA across 12 starts and 71.1 innings, but his recent form is the reason the under sits at minus 135. Over his last three starts Baz has gone seven, seven, and seven innings, allowing one, one, and two earned runs, including a nine-strikeout gem against Tampa Bay on May 26 and a two-run, seven-inning outing against Boston on June 2. A starter going seven and giving up two or fewer in three straight is doing the heavy lifting on a team-total under by himself. Against a Jays lineup that has been held under five in the majority of its recent games, under 4.5 is the disciplined number even at the steeper juice.

Colorado Rockies Under 4.5: The Coors Caveat And Why The Number Still Works

Colorado is always the trickiest team-total to bet because the park bakes a premium into everything, and we are not going to pretend Coors Field is not in the equation. The Rockies have averaged 4.39 runs per game over their last 28, the highest of the four teams here, and their recent log is loud, with several games of seven, eight, and nine in the mix. So why is the under 4.5 at minus 105 still a number we respect? Because the games where they erupt are sitting next to games of one and four, and the opposing arm Sunday is the best of the four on paper.

Milwaukee runs Shane Drohan out there, and Drohan has been excellent in limited 2026 work: a 2.87 ERA, a 1.15 WHIP, and 33 strikeouts in 31.1 innings for a 9.48 strikeout-per-nine. His recent outings have been short but stingy, including a scoreless four-inning start against the Cubs and a one-run effort against St. Louis. A high-strikeout arm at altitude is the one profile that can survive Coors, because the ball you do not put in play cannot find the gaps. At minus 105 you are getting a near-even price on a team-total under backed by the best pitcher form on the board, with the park risk already reflected in the lean price. That is the trade-off you take.

How To Play A Team-Total Board Like This

The honest read on a day with four of these is that they are not all the same bet. The Tigers under 3.5 is the one with the cleanest signal, because the line steam and the run rate are independent pieces of evidence pointing the same direction, and you are getting plus money to boot. The Astros and Rockies unders are volatility plays, where the average sits near the number but the under hit rate and the opposing arm tilt it. The Blue Jays under is the pitcher-driven one, riding three straight strong Baz starts at the steepest price of the group. Rank them that way, size them that way, and do not talk yourself into treating a minus 135 the same as a plus 100. The market handed you a tell in Detroit this morning. The rest of the board is about whether the run rates and the arms agree, and on this Sunday, more often than not, they do.
