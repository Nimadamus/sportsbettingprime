NHL Monday

Tortorella's Vegas Debut, Celebrini's 100-Point Watch, and Leafs Face Elimination

March 30, 2026 | 8 min read | Sports Betting Prime

Monday night's five-game NHL slate is absolutely loaded with storylines that matter. John Tortorella steps behind a new bench for the first time in three years. An 18-year-old is two points away from joining Wayne Gretzky in the record books. A franchise that hasn't won a playoff series since 2004 is staring at elimination with eight games still to play. And in the Metro, a single point separates two teams fighting for second place. This is what the stretch run is all about.

Let's break down every game on the board tonight, from the betting lines to the storylines driving them.

One point. That's all that separates these two clubs in the Metropolitan Division standings, and the fact that this game is happening on a random Monday night in late March makes it feel like a playoff preview. New York sits at 89 points, Pittsburgh at 88, and both are jockeying for second place in a division where positioning could mean the difference between facing a wildcard team or a divisional buzzsaw in Round 1.

The elephant in the room is Sidney Crosby's status. He's listed as a game-time decision with a lower body issue, and with Evgeni Malkin already out for a fourth consecutive game, the Penguins could be rolling into this one without both of their franchise cornerstones. That's a massive problem against an Islanders team that thrives on structure and suffocating defense under Patrick Roy. Pittsburgh has lost three of their last four, and the timing couldn't be worse.

Ilya Sorokin is the kind of goaltender who elevates in big moments, and this qualifies. The Islanders have been one of the more consistent home teams in the second half, and the Penguins asking Arturs Silovs to steal a game of this magnitude is a tall order. The total at 6 feels right for a game where both teams will likely play tight, protect leads, and limit odd-man rushes. But the real story here is the Crosby decision. If he sits, Pittsburgh becomes a much different animal.

Nathan MacKinnon is putting together one of the most complete individual seasons we've seen in years. He leads the entire NHL with 48 goals and 117 points, and the Rocket Richard Trophy race against Cole Caufield (44 goals) is becoming must-watch television down the stretch. Every time MacKinnon steps on the ice at Ball Arena, the crowd knows they might be watching something historic.

Here's the wrinkle, though: Colorado has actually lost three of their last four at home. That's a bizarre stat for a team sitting at 106 points, but it's real. The Avalanche have been coasting at times, perhaps already looking ahead to the postseason with their spot locked up. Calgary isn't in the playoff picture at 70 points, but Dustin Wolf in net gives them a fighting chance on any given night. Wolf has been one of the few bright spots for the Flames this season, and young goalies with nothing to lose can be dangerous opponents for teams in cruise control.

The 10-game point streak Colorado carries at home against Calgary is an impressive historical data point, but the recent stumble is worth noting. The puck line at COL -1.5 (-139) suggests the market expects a multi-goal win, but those three recent home losses should give you pause. This feels like a game where MacKinnon gets his, but the margin might be tighter than -347 implies. Scott Wedgewood gets the start in net for the Avs, another indicator that this is a maintenance game rather than a full-throttle effort.

This might be the most consequential game on the board tonight, even if it doesn't have the flashiest individual storyline. Toronto's season is hanging by a thread. If the Leafs lose in regulation tonight AND the Islanders get at least a point in their game against Pittsburgh, Toronto is mathematically eliminated from the playoffs. Let that sink in. The franchise that hasn't won a Cup since 1967, that has tormented its fans for decades with heartbreaking early exits, might not even make the dance this year.

The Ducks are first in the Pacific at 86 points, and they've been one of the league's best stories all season. Anaheim at home is a tough draw for anyone, and they're not going to take their foot off the gas with the division lead still up for grabs. The total at 6.5 is the highest on the board tonight, which makes sense given both teams' offensive tendencies.

There's one glimmer of hope for Toronto: they've won six straight against Anaheim dating back to the 2022-23 season. That's a dominant stretch against a specific opponent, and sometimes that kind of matchup comfort matters, especially when a team's back is against the wall. Desperation can be a powerful motivator, and the Leafs know exactly what's at stake tonight. But Anaheim isn't the pushover they used to be. This Ducks team is built to compete, and they're not going to hand Toronto anything.

The moneyline at TOR +163 reflects a Toronto team that most people have written off. If you believe in desperation hockey, that number has some appeal. But the smarter approach might be watching the Penguins-Islanders result first, because if Pittsburgh wins in regulation, Toronto gets a lifeline regardless of what happens here.

Forget the records. Forget that both teams are sitting at 73 points in the Western Conference basement. This game is about one thing: Macklin Celebrini chasing history.

The San Jose Sharks' 18-year-old phenom has 98 points (36 goals, 62 assists) in 71 games this season. He needs just two more points to become the sixth teenager in NHL history to reach the 100-point mark. The names on that list? Wayne Gretzky. Mario Lemieux. Sidney Crosby. Jimmy Carson. Dale Hawerchuk. That's it. Celebrini is on an eight-game point streak and looks completely dialed in. Every shift he takes tonight could be the one that puts him in the history books alongside the greatest players to ever lace them up.

The Blues, though, aren't going to roll over. St. Louis has won four straight and their defensive effort over the last 10 games has been remarkable, allowing just 1.4 goals per game. That's elite-level shutdown hockey, and it's a direct contrast to San Jose's recent struggles, as the Sharks have gone 3-6-1 over their last 10 while surrendering 3.7 goals per game. The moneyline is essentially a pick'em at STL -104 / SJS -102, which tells you the market respects what the Blues have been doing lately.

The under at 6 (-116) is intriguing given St. Louis' defensive surge, but Celebrini in his home building chasing a milestone has a way of making things happen. This is the kind of game where the atmosphere alone could carry San Jose past their recent struggles. The Sharks want to give their franchise cornerstone something to remember, and the building will be electric from puck drop.

History Watch: If Macklin Celebrini reaches 100 points tonight, he would join Wayne Gretzky (1979-80, 137 PTS), Mario Lemieux (1984-85, 100 PTS), Sidney Crosby (2005-06, 102 PTS), Jimmy Carson (1987-88, 107 PTS), and Dale Hawerchuk (1981-82, 103 PTS) as the only teenagers in NHL history to accomplish the feat.

This is the one everyone's watching tonight. John Tortorella stands behind the Vegas Golden Knights' bench for the first time after the organization fired Bruce Cassidy on March 29 following a disastrous 8-15-4 stretch since January 19. Torts brings 770 career coaching wins, a Stanley Cup from 2004 with Tampa Bay, and a gold medal from the 2026 Winter Olympics with Team USA. He's one of the most polarizing, intense, demanding coaches in hockey history, and Vegas just handed him the keys with eight games left in the regular season.

The Golden Knights are sitting at 80 points, still clinging to playoff hopes in the Western Conference. They have the talent, particularly with Mitch Marner in the lineup after his trade from Toronto last July. But the system under Cassidy had completely broken down, and the question now is whether Tortorella can install enough structure in a single practice to change the trajectory of this season. History says the "new coach bump" is real, and Vegas at home against an eliminated Vancouver team is about as soft a landing as you could ask for a debut.

Vancouver is mathematically eliminated and playing out the string at 67 points. The Canucks have nothing to play for beyond pride and auditions for next season. That's typically a recipe for flat, disengaged hockey, and the market has priced this accordingly with Vegas at -311. The total at 6.5 suggests the books expect some offense, which makes sense for a game where one team might be running on adrenaline and the other team might not care all that much about defensive assignments.

The real fascination here isn't the betting angle, it's the scene. T-Mobile Arena is going to be rocking for Tortorella's debut. The players will be desperate to prove they deserve to keep their jobs under a coach known for holding everyone accountable. This could be a rout, or it could be a tense, sloppy affair as the Golden Knights try to figure out a new system on the fly. Either way, it's appointment viewing.

Monday's Biggest Takeaways for the Stretch Run

Tonight's slate is a microcosm of everything that makes the NHL's final two weeks so compelling. You've got a new coach trying to save a season in Vegas, a teenager on the verge of joining a list that starts with Gretzky, a storied franchise facing elimination on the road, and a Metro Division race that could swing on a single goal in Long Island.

The betting board reflects all of that chaos. The Penguins-Islanders game is tight for a reason. The Blues-Sharks pick'em is a reminder that form matters more than records in late March. And the Leafs at +163 is the kind of number that either looks brilliant or predictable by midnight.

Whatever you're watching tonight, you won't be bored. This is the NHL at its best.
