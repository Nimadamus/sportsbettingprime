2026 NHL Stanley Cup Final Odds Preview: Hurricanes Favored Over Golden Knights as Andersen Carries Carolina

May 30, 2026 | NHL | Sports Betting Prime

It is finally set. The Carolina Hurricanes will host the Vegas Golden Knights in the 2026 Stanley Cup Final, a matchup that pits the East's relentless, structured wagon against a Western champion that just ripped through the Colorado Avalanche in a sweep. The oddsmakers have wasted no time picking a side. Carolina opens as the title favorite at roughly minus 145 in the series price, with Vegas hovering around plus 130, and Game 1 lands in Raleigh on Tuesday night with the Hurricanes installed as the home chalk. Numbers like that do not happen by accident, and the story behind them runs straight through the Carolina crease.

Why the Hurricanes Open as Stanley Cup Final Favorites

For a franchise that had spent years bowing out a round or two short, this is a genuine return to the stage. Carolina is back in the Stanley Cup Final for the first time since 2006, the year the Hurricanes lifted the Cup with Rod Brind'Amour wearing the captain's C. Two decades later Brind'Amour is the one running the bench, and the symmetry is not lost on anyone in Raleigh. The Hurricanes carved their path the way they always promise to, grinding through the Senators, the Flyers, and finally the Canadiens, whom they closed out with a 6-1 hammering in Game 5 of the Eastern Conference Final. That is three series handled without a single one stretching to a seventh game, which is exactly the kind of profile that earns a team a favorite's price in the championship round.

The market reflects that resume. With Carolina sitting near minus 145 and the implied series probability landing in the low 60s, sportsbooks are telling you the Hurricanes are the more complete and more battle-tested club at the moment the puck drops. The series spread underlines the same belief, with Carolina priced as a sizable favorite to win in six or fewer and Vegas carrying value only if the underdog can stretch this thing toward a Game 7. None of that means the number is unbeatable, but it does tell you where the sharp money expects the structural edge to live.

Frederik Andersen Has Been the MVP of Carolina's Run

If you want the single biggest reason the Hurricanes are favored, look no further than the man between the pipes. Frederik Andersen has been the difference-maker of this entire postseason, posting a sub-1.50 goals-against average with a save percentage around .928 across a stretch where he piled up wins in nearly every start he made. Those are not good playoff numbers, they are elite ones, the sort of run that turns a strong team into a favorite and a goaltender into the early conversation for the most valuable player of the playoffs. When a netminder is stopping pucks at that clip in May, every other variable in a series shrinks.

That matters even more in a Cup Final, where margins compress and a single soft goal can decide a game. Andersen has given Carolina a floor that very few teams can match, allowing the Hurricanes to play their heavy, forechecking style without sweating the occasional defensive breakdown. A goalie playing at this level lets a club lean into its identity, and Carolina's identity is suffocating opponents and trusting the man behind them to clean up the rest. As long as Andersen holds anything close to this form, the Hurricanes will be the harder team to score on in this series, and that is the foundation the favorite's number is built on.

The Golden Knights Earned This With a Sweep of the Avalanche

None of this makes Vegas a pushover, and the plus 130 price is a reminder of that. The Golden Knights did not back into the Final, they swept the Colorado Avalanche to get here, eliminating the West's heavyweight in four games to punch their ticket. A team that can dispose of a roster as talented as Colorado's that decisively carries real teeth, and Vegas has been here before, with a championship pedigree and a roster that knows how to win the games that matter most. Underestimating a club that just rolled the Avalanche would be a mistake, and the betting market has priced them as a live underdog rather than a sacrificial lamb.

Vegas brings its own dangerous pieces, from Mark Stone's playoff scoring history to a goaltender in Carter Hart who has carried the Golden Knights all the way through this run. The Western champion will also arrive with rest and rhythm after closing out Colorado without the wear of a long series. The question is whether that firepower can solve a Carolina structure that has not cracked, and whether anyone in this matchup can consistently beat Andersen when it counts. That is the central tension of the series, and it is why the plus 130 on Vegas is the kind of price that will tempt anyone who believes goaltending hot streaks eventually cool.

Betting Angles for the 2026 Stanley Cup Final

The shape of this series is fairly clear from the opening numbers. Carolina is the favorite because it is deeper, more tested, and riding the best goaltender left standing, while Vegas is the live dog with championship experience and a sweep on its resume. For bettors weighing the series price, the decision comes down to a familiar question: do you pay the Carolina tax and trust the structure plus the goalie, or do you take the value on a battle-hardened Vegas team getting plus money? The Game 7 angle is where Vegas's number looks most attractive, since the underdog's series equity climbs sharply if the Knights can simply extend the series rather than win it outright.

The most important variable to track game by game is Andersen. If his save percentage holds anywhere near where it has been, totals are likely to skew toward the under and Carolina's puck-line price becomes more interesting in the games it controls. If Vegas can finally start solving him, the entire complexion of the series, and its prices, will shift in a hurry. This is a Final that the market believes Carolina should win, but it is also one where a single hot or cold week from either goaltender could rewrite the whole story. Game 1 in Raleigh on Tuesday night is the first real test of whether the favorite's number is built on solid ice.
